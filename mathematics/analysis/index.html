Axiom 2.1.1

0 is a natural number.

Axiom 2.1.2

If n is a natural number, then n++ is also a natural number

Axiom 2.1.3

0 is not the successor of any natural number; i.e., we have n++ î€ = 0 for every natural number n.

Axiom 2.1.4.

Different natural number must have different successors; i.e., if n, m are natural number and n î€ = m, then n++ î€ = m++. Equivalently, if n++ = m++, then we must have n = m.

Axiom 2.1.5. (Principle of mathematical induction)

Let P(n) be any property pertaining to a natural number n. Suppose that P(0) is true, and suppose that whenever P(n) is true, P(n++) is also true. Then P(n) is true for every natural number n.

Lemma 2.2.2.

For any natural number n, n+0=n.

Proof.

The base case 0+0=0 follows since we know that 0+m=m for every natural number m, and 0 is a natural number. Now suppose inductively that n+0=n. We wish to show that (n++)+0=n++. But by the definition of addition, (n++)+0 is equal to (n+0)++, which is equal to n++ since n+0=n. This closes the induction.

Lemma 2.2.3.

For any natural number n and m, n+(m++)=(n+m)++.

Proof.

We will use induction in n (keeping m fixed), we must prove the base case 0+(m++)=(0+m)++. But by the definition of addition 0+(m++)=m++ and 0+m=m, so both sides of the base case are equal to m++ and are thus equal to each other. Now, we assume inductively that n+(m++)=(n+m)++. We wish to show that (n++)+(m++)=((n++)+m)++. By the definition of addition, the LHS is equal to (n+(m++))++ and is equal to ((n+m)++)++ by the inductive hypothesis. And the RHS is equal to ((n+m)++)++ by the definition of addition. Thus both sides are equal to each other. This closes the induction.

Proposition 2.2.4. (Addition is commutative).

For any natural numbers n and m, n+m=m+n.

Proof.

We will use induction in n (keeping m fixed). We must prove the base case 0+m=m+0. By definition of addition, the LHS equal to m, and by Lemma 2.2.2, the RHS equal to m, so both sides of the base case are equal to m and are thus equal to each other. Now, we assume inductively that n+m=m+n. We wish to show that (n++)+m=m+(n++). By definition of addition, the LHS become (n+m)++, and by Lemma 2.2.3, the RHS become (m+n)++, but by the inductive hypothesis n+m=m+n. Thus (n+m)++=(m+n)++. This closes the induction.

Proposition 2.2.5. (Addition is associative).

For any natural number a, b, c, we have (a+b)+c=a+(b+c).

Proof. (Exercise 2.2.1)

We will use induction in b (keeping a,c fixed). We must prove the base case (a+0)+c=a+(0+c). By Lemma 2.2.2., the LHS is equal to a+c, and by the Definition of addition, the RHS is equal to a+c, so both sides of the base case are equal to a+c, and are thus equal to each other. Now, we assume inductively that (a+b)+c=a+(b+c). We wish to show that (a+b++)+c=a+((b++)+). By Lemma 2.2.3, the LHS become ((a+b)++)+c, and by Definition of addition, the LHS is equal to ((a+b)+c)++. By Definition of addition, the RHS become a+(b+c)++, and by Lemma 2.2.3, the RHS is equal to (a+(b+c))++. But by the inductive hypothesis (a+b)+c=a+(b+c). Thus, ((a+b)+c)++=(a+(b+c))++. This closes the induction.

Proposition 2.2.6. (Cancellation law).

Let a, b, c be natural numbers such that a+b=a+c. Then we have b=c.

Proof.

We will use induction in a (keeping b,c fixed). We must prove the base case if 0+b=0+c then b=c. By the Definition of addition, 0+b=b, and 0+c=c, so b=c . Now, we assume inductively that if a+b=a+c then b=c. We wish to show that if (a++)+b=(a++)+c then b=c. By the Definition of addition, the LHS is equal to (a+b)++, the RHS is equal to (a+c)++. But by the inductive hypothesis if we have a+b=a+c then we have b=c which leads to b++=c++. By Axiom 2.1.4. and we already have b++=c++. Thus, b=c. This closes the induction.

Proposition 2.2.8.

If a is positive and b is a natural number, then a+b is positive (and hence b+a is also, by Proposition 2.2.4.)

Proof.

We will use induction in b. If b=0, then a+b=a+0=a, which is positive, so this proves the base case. Now suppose inductively that a+b is positive. Then a+(b++)=(a+b)++ is positive due to Axiom 2.1.3. This closes the induction.
